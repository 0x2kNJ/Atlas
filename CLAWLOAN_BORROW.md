# ClawLoan × Atlas — Use Case: AI Agent Credit (Borrow)

**Document type:** Use case brief  
**Audience:** ClawLoan team · Potential investors · Protocol developers  
**Demo:** `localhost:5173 → ClawLoan → Borrow`

---

## The Problem ClawLoan Has Today

ClawLoan extends uncollateralized micro-credit to AI agents based on credit history. The value proposition is straightforward: agents that have repaid reliably in the past can borrow again without posting collateral.

But the model has a structural dependency that limits how far it can scale:

**Repayment requires the agent to be online at deadline.**

If the agent key is rate-limited by its provider, compromised, shut down, or simply delayed at the wrong moment, the loan goes unpaid. The lender has no collateral to fall back on and no way to enforce repayment without the agent's active participation. This caps the credit limits lenders are willing to extend and the number of agents they'll onboard.

The problem isn't the agent's intent. It's that the enforcement mechanism — "the agent will submit the repayment transaction" — depends on a server being alive at a specific moment in time.

---

## What Atlas Adds: Liveness-Independent Enforcement

Atlas replaces "the agent will repay" with "the repayment is cryptographically pre-committed."

Before the agent goes about its work, it pre-authorizes a repayment intent and locks it in an Atlas Envelope. The envelope encumbers the repayment position in a singleton vault and registers an on-chain condition: if the loan deadline arrives, any keeper can trigger the repayment automatically. The agent does not need to be online. No trusted party needs to exist. The execution is permissionless and deterministic.

The lender's guarantee changes from "we trust the agent will repay" to "a keeper will execute this pre-signed repayment when the deadline clock hits, regardless of whether the agent is alive."

## Demo Flow: Five Steps

### Step 1 — Mint & Deposit
The agent mints 1,000 USDC (simulating accumulated earnings) and deposits it into the Atlas SingletonVault. The deposit creates a cryptographic position commitment: `keccak256(owner, asset, amount, salt)`. No per-user contract is deployed. The position is a hash.

### Step 2 — Borrow
The agent submits a credit proof to ClawLoan's `MockCreditGatedLender`. The lender verifies the agent's credit tier (ZK-certified grade) and releases 500 USDC. The agent now holds the borrowed USDC and has an outstanding obligation.

### Step 3 — Sign & Register the Repayment Envelope
The agent signs three EIP-712 messages in a single session:
1. **SpendCapability** — authorizes the ClawloanRepayAdapter to spend up to 505 USDC from the vault position
2. **Intent** — specifies the exact repayment: 505 USDC → ClawloanRepayAdapter → repay bot #99's debt
3. **ManageCapability** — delegates registration authority to the EnvelopeRegistry

The envelope is registered on-chain. The vault position is **encumbered** — it cannot be withdrawn or spent by any other intent until the envelope is resolved. The deadline condition is set.

**The agent can now go offline. Everything from here requires zero agent liveness.**

### Step 4 — Keeper Triggers (Agent Offline)
A keeper monitors the EnvelopeRegistry. When the loan deadline arrives, it calls `trigger()`. The registry reads the timestamp oracle, verifies the condition is met, and forwards execution to the CapabilityKernel. The kernel verifies all signatures, releases the vault position, routes it through the ClawloanRepayAdapter, and repays the debt on the agent's behalf. The keeper earns a small reward from the envelope's `keeperRewardBps`.

The lender receives principal + interest. The cycle is complete. No agent signature was required at execution time.

### Step 5 — ZK Credit Upgrade
After successful repayment, the agent's compliance history is updated. A ZK proof (generated by the receipt accumulator circuit) attests to N repayments, all on-time, without revealing any individual loan detail. This proof upgrades the agent to a higher credit tier, unlocking larger future borrowing limits. The trust is built from cryptographic evidence, not from a lender deciding to trust the agent's word.

## What This Looks Like in the Demo

| Moment | What happens | What Atlas is doing |
|:--|:--|:--|
| Step 3 complete | Agent is "offline" | Position encumbered in vault; envelope active |
| Deadline arrives | Keeper fires `trigger()` | Kernel verifies pre-signed intent; adapter repays |
| Repayment lands | Lender balance credited | No agent participation in final execution |
| ZK proof issued | Credit tier increases | Compliance proven without revealing loan details |

The demo shows both sides: the borrower experience (single setup session, then offline) and the lender experience (automated repayment arrives without the agent being reachable).

## Without Atlas vs. With Atlas

| Dimension | Without Atlas | With Atlas |
|:--|:--|:--|
| Repayment mechanism | Agent must be online at deadline | Pre-committed; keeper fires permissionlessly |
| Key compromise impact | Agent drained, loan unpaid | Capability bounds cap loss to repayment amount |
| Lender confidence | Trust in agent's operational reliability | Trust in the cryptographic commitment |
| Credit limit ceiling | Low — limited by agent uptime risk | Higher — liveness risk is removed from the model |
| Credit history | Lender stores repayment record | ZK proof of compliance history; portable, privacy-preserving |

## What ClawLoan Gains From This Integration

1. **Higher credit limits.** Lenders who know repayment is enforced regardless of agent liveness can offer larger loans. The risk model improves structurally, not incrementally.

2. **Institutional lender onboarding.** Institutional capital providers require enforceable repayment guarantees, not "we trust the bot." The Atlas envelope provides exactly that — a cryptographic commitment they can evaluate independently.

3. **Reduced counterparty risk.** Each loan's repayment obligation is isolated to a specific vault position. A single defaulting agent cannot affect another agent's position or the pool's general liquidity.

4. **Composable credit history.** The ZK compliance circuit generates portable proofs. An agent's repayment history on ClawLoan becomes a trust signal usable across any protocol that accepts Atlas compliance proofs.

## Technical Integration Points

The integration requires two components ClawLoan does not currently ship:

**1. `ClawloanRepayAdapter`** (deployed)  
Implements `IAdapter`. Receives USDC from the kernel, calls `MockClawloanPool.repay(botId, amount)`, returns remaining USDC to the vault. Stateless. No persistent storage.

**2. Timestamp oracle at `EnvelopeRegistry.trigger()`**  
The deadline condition reads `block.timestamp` via a Chainlink-compatible oracle. No off-chain component. The keeper monitors block time, not an API.

Both are live in the current demo deployment. The adapter code is auditable at `contracts/adapters/ClawloanRepayAdapter.sol`.

## What We're Asking ClawLoan For

**Ask (a) — Feature experimentation:**  
Access to a ClawLoan testnet deployment where the `repay()` function can be called by an address other than the borrowing agent — specifically, by the Atlas kernel acting on behalf of the pre-signed intent. This is a permissioning change at the ClawLoan contract level, not at the Atlas level.

**Ask (b) — Primitive development:**  
Collaboration on the `CreditVerifier` interface. Currently mocked. A production integration would use ZK proof verification from ClawLoan's credit scoring system as the tier assignment mechanism. We need ClawLoan's proof schema to build the compliant circuit.

**Ask (c) — Investment signal:**  
The ClawLoan × Atlas borrow flow is the clearest single demonstration of what a liveness-independent lending market looks like. This is the demo we show institutional lenders to explain why the Atlas enforcement layer matters at the product level.
